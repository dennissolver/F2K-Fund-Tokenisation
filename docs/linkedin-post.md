# LinkedIn Post — Property Development Tokenisation

---

What if investors could subscribe to a housing fund with USDC and receive security tokens representing their units — with NAV published on-chain weekly and distributions paid quarterly to their wallets?

That's exactly what we built.

Over the past few months, I've been developing a tokenised housing fund platform as a proof of concept for how property development financing could work on blockchain. Not as a whitepaper exercise — as a working system.

Here's what the stack looks like:

- ERC-3643 security tokens (the regulated token standard) that enforce investor verification at the smart contract level. If your wallet isn't on the allowlist, you can't hold the token. Period.

- A full admin console where fund managers confirm subscriptions, mint tokens, publish NAV, execute distributions, and manage allowlists — with every action audit-logged and requiring dual approval.

- Smart contracts handling three core fund operations: USDC subscriptions routed to a Gnosis Safe treasury, weekly NAV attestation stored immutably on-chain, and pro-rata quarterly distributions executed in a single transaction.

- KYC/AML built into the onboarding flow, with on-chain identity registration gating token transfers.

- Asset staking where manufacturers and investors submit property, equipment, or other collateral for valuation, with configurable LTV ratios and concentration risk monitoring across asset classes.

The point isn't the specific fund. It's that the infrastructure for tokenised property development now exists and it's not theoretical.

A regulated Managed Investment Scheme can run on smart contracts. Wholesale investors can subscribe with stablecoins. Compliance can be enforced on-chain, not just in a PDF. And fund operations that traditionally take days of manual reconciliation can be reduced to a few clicks with a full audit trail.

The Australian housing market needs $50B+ in new construction financing over the next decade. Tokenisation won't solve the housing crisis on its own — but it opens up capital formation pathways that didn't exist five years ago.
